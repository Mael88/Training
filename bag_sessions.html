Knee Weakness — It Is the Bag, Not You
The knee feeling weak on the bag is a surface problem, not a technique problem. A hanging heavy bag swings away from the knee on impact because the contact angle is vertical rather than forward. Knees feeling stronger when you grip and pull the bag confirms the technique is correct — that grip simulates the clinch resistance that makes knees devastating. No technique fix needed here.
Bag Swing on Mawashi Combos
After the roundhouse, step off the line of the bag’s swing before launching punches. The bag arcs predictably — step to the side of that arc rather than staying directly in front of it. Built into the combinations this week.

Foundation

2
Kicks — Distance + Bag Angle Correction
Kick Sharpness
Mae geri and mawashi geri — both legs. Distance reset step before every kick.
Lead mawashi geri cue: drive the hip forward and laterally into the kick. Do not let the torso tilt back as the leg rises. The hip leads — the leg follows. Torso stays upright throughout.
Mawashi geri + punches — bag swing fix: after the roundhouse lands, step to the side of the bag’s swing arc before launching punches. The bag will swing predictably. Do not stand directly in its path — step off the line and attack from a new angle.
3
Knees — Grip and Drive
Knee Strike Refinement
Hiza geri with bag grip only this round. Grab the bag at shoulder height, pull it toward you, drive 2–3 knees in, release, step back, reset.
On the knee feeling weak: this is the bag surface, not your technique. The grip-and-pull simulates the clinch resistance that makes the knee land properly. Without that resistance, the bag just swings away. Trust the technique — when you feel the bag pulled toward you and the knee meets it mid-drive, that is correct.
Hip drive direction: the knee goes straight up and slightly forward into the target. Not a wide arc — a short, sharp vertical drive. Think of driving your kneecap through the bag, not swinging your leg at it.
The knee will feel much more powerful in a real application with a partner holding a pad or in a clinch. The bag’s swing limitation is a training surface issue, not a technique issue. Do not try to compensate for it by changing the mechanics.
4
Full Combination Flow — Chosen Sequences
Consolidation Round
Pick your two best-feeling combinations from this mesocycle and drill them alternately this round. Clean, sharp, with footwork between each.
Suggestion based on your feedback: mae geri → jab-cross → rear hiza geri felt the most natural. Use that as one. For the second, pick whichever punch combination flows best — jab-cross-lead hook or jab-cross-jab.
Quality marker: by the end of this round, each combination should feel like a single connected movement rather than a sequence of separate strikes. That is the taper goal — consolidation of the patterns built across 5 weeks.

Full Cycle Mistakes Summary

Full mesocycle reference
Complete mistakes reference for all strikes introduced across Mesocycle 4. Keep this as a reference going into the deload and next cycle.
Updated HR Zones — Confirmed Max HR 190
  • Z2: 114–133 bpm — conversational, sustainable
  • Z3: 133–152 bpm — breathing harder, still controlled
  • Z4: 152–171 bpm — uncomfortable, can’t talk

Universal Corrections — Across All Strikes
  • Shoulder blade down on all punches and hooks: push the blade down and forward as each strike launches. This is now automatic — the fix for left trap overactivation and arm-too-far-from-body.
  • Guard stays up during all strikes: every combination ends with both hands at cheek height. No exceptions.
  • Distance management: reset step back before kicks. No reset before knees — knees belong at close range after punches close the gap.
  • Hip reset between consecutive same-side strikes: heel returns to floor between two crosses. Full rotation out, full recovery back.
