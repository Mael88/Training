1
Punch Warm-Up — Shoulder Blade Active
Foundation Before Kicks
Full punch toolkit — jab, cross, hooks. 60–70% effort. Warm everything up before the new material arrives.
Shoulder blade active on every strike: cross, lead hook, rear hook — the cue is the same. Push the blade down and forward. If the shoulder rides up, the arm wanders away from the body and the load transfers to the wrong place.
Jab-lead hook and cross-rear hook: include both this round. They feel awkward — keep working them. The hip transitions will click with repetition.
2
Mae Geri — Front Kick Isolation
NEW
New — Mae Geri (Front Kick)
In atemi-waza and karate: mae geri — a thrusting kick delivered with the ball of the foot (koshi) or heel. Primary function: distance control and disrupting an opponent's posture before closing. Your karate background makes this familiar territory.
Chamber first: before the kick extends, the knee drives upward toward the chest — this is the chamber. Do not skip it. The power and accuracy of the kick come from the chamber, not from swinging the leg forward.
Extension: from the chamber, the leg drives forward and extends. The hip drives into the kick — same principle as the cross. The supporting leg stays slightly bent, not locked.
Impact surface: ball of the foot (toes pulled back) for a snapping version, or heel for a thrusting version. On a heavy bag, heel is safer on the wrists — use it until technique is solid.
Recovery: after the kick, the foot does not drop straight to the floor — it returns through the chamber position before landing. This maintains balance and prevents the kick from being grabbed.
Guard stays up: both hands remain at cheek height during the kick. The upper body does not lean back to compensate for the kick.
Common error: skipping the chamber and swinging the leg forward. This produces a weak, slow kick with no hip engagement. Chamber — then kick.
Lead leg mae geri x3 → reset → rear leg mae geri x3 → reset → repeat.
3
Mae Geri — Build Commitment
KICK
Front Kick — More Intent
Same mechanics as Round 2. Now add more hip drive into the kick — push the hip forward as the leg extends. This is the difference between a tap and a kick that actually moves a person.
Balance check: after each kick, you should be able to hold the chamber position for 1–2 seconds without toppling. If you can't, the supporting leg and core are not stabilising the movement. Slow down.
Your judo background gives you good single-leg stability. Trust it. The instability in kicks usually comes from rushing the extension before the chamber is set — not from lack of base.
Rear leg vs lead leg: the rear leg kick is more powerful (more hip rotation available). The lead leg kick is faster (less wind-up). Use both this round.
4
Mawashi Geri — Roundhouse Isolation
NEW
New — Mawashi Geri (Roundhouse)
In atemi-waza: mawashi geri — a circular kick targeting the thigh, ribs, or head depending on height. The hip rotation is the engine, exactly as in the hook. The arc of the leg mirrors the arc of the hook — same lateral hip snap, different weapon.
Chamber: knee drives upward and outward to the side — not straight forward as in mae geri. The knee points in the direction the kick will travel before the leg extends.
Hip pivot: the supporting foot pivots as the hip rotates into the kick. This is non-negotiable — if the supporting foot does not pivot, the hip is blocked and the kick has no power. Same principle as the rear heel pivot in the cross.
Impact surface: the instep (top of the foot) or shin. On the bag, shin is safer and more powerful — it is what Muay Thai fighters use and what works in atemi-waza for body targeting.
Target this week: mid-level — body height. Do not attempt head-level kicks yet. Get the mechanics right at body height first.
Recovery: foot returns through the chamber before landing, same as mae geri.
Common error: the supporting foot does not pivot. The result is a kick that uses only the hip flexor and has no rotational power. Feel the supporting heel pivot outward as the kick lands.
Lead leg mawashi geri x3 → reset → rear leg mawashi geri x3 → reset → repeat.
5
Mae Geri + Mawashi Geri — Alternating
KICKS
Connecting Both Kicks
Alternate front kick and roundhouse. Feel the difference between the two — mae geri is linear (hip drives forward), mawashi geri is rotational (hip snaps laterally).
Both legs: work both lead and rear leg for each kick. The lead leg versions are faster and shorter — the rear leg versions are slower but more powerful.
Guard stays up: this is the moment most people forget the hands. Both fists stay at cheek height during every kick. The upper body does not tilt or lean to compensate.
Think of the bag as an opponent at mid distance. The kicks are meant to damage, disrupt, and control that distance — not just to hit something. Stay in your stance and reset your guard after each kick.
Mae geri → reset → mawashi geri → reset → alternate legs → repeat.
6
Punch-Kick Preview — Cross + Mae Geri
First Punch-Kick Connection
Cross followed by rear leg mae geri. One cross, one kick — no more. Keep it deliberate at RPE 6.
Logic: the cross disrupts and closes distance slightly. The front kick immediately re-establishes distance and continues pressure. In atemi-waza this is a fundamental distance management sequence.
Connection: after the cross lands and the guard returns, chamber the rear leg immediately. Do not pause in between — the cross and kick should feel like a continuous sequence with one brief reset.
If the kick loses all mechanics after the cross — no chamber, no hip drive — separate the two and drill them independently before reconnecting. Do not train a sloppy kick just to complete the combination.
Cross → rear mae geri → land → reset guard → step back → repeat.

Coaching Notes

You have the kicks from Tuesday. Today you integrate them with punches — short combinations first, then building. This is the first true atemi-waza session: upper and lower body weapons working together. RPE 8 is the target — this session is meant to be hard.

  • 10 hip circles each side — wide and slow

Main Work — 8 Rounds
2 min rounds · 60 sec rest · RPE 8 · Full atemi-waza toolkit. Push the output.
1
Punch Toolkit — Re-Establish
Full punch toolkit at 70% effort. Jab, cross, lead hook, rear hook. Re-establish the mechanics before kicks enter.
Shoulder blade active on every strike. Set it before the round starts and maintain it throughout.
2
Kick Warm-Up — Both Kicks, Both Legs
KICKS
Mae geri and mawashi geri, both legs, 60% effort. Warm the kicking mechanics before integrating.
Chamber check: is the chamber happening before the kick extends? Pivot check: is the supporting foot turning on the roundhouse? Establish both before Round 3.
3
Jab-Cross + Mae Geri
COMBO
First Full Punch-Kick Combination
Structure: jab-cross (punch entry) → rear leg mae geri (kick to create distance). Two punches to disrupt, one kick to control distance and add damage.
Transition: after the cross returns to guard, chamber the rear leg immediately. The guard is up during the kick — do not drop the hands as the leg rises.
70% effort this round — feel the transition from punching to kicking before pushing the output.
If the kick loses its chamber after the punches, the legs are not yet coordinated with the punch-kick transition. Slow down — two punches, pause, then kick. Rebuild the connection gradually.
Jab-cross → mae geri → land → reset → repeat.
4
Jab-Cross + Mae Geri — Push RPE 8
COMBO
Same combination. Push to RPE 8. Move after every sequence.
Intent: the punches disrupt, the kick damages and resets distance. Every combination should feel purposeful — not just hitting the bag, but executing a tactical sequence.
The bag will swing significantly after the kick. Step off the line as it comes back — do not stand in front of a swinging bag. Reset your position, attack again from a new angle.
5
Mae Geri + Jab-Cross
COMBO
Kick Opening the Combination
Reverse the order — kick first, then punches. Mae geri (front kick) → jab-cross.
Atemi-waza logic: the front kick disrupts the opponent's posture and creates an opening — the punches follow immediately into that opening before they recover. The kick is the door opener here; the punches do the damage.
Recovery after the kick: the kicking foot lands and immediately resets into your fighting stance. The punches launch from that stance — not from a compromised position mid-landing.
Mae geri → land in stance → jab-cross → reset → repeat.
6
Jab-Cross + Mawashi Geri
COMBO
Roundhouse Finishing the Combination
Jab-cross → rear leg mawashi geri (roundhouse to the body). Same punch entry as Round 3, different kick finish.
Hip pivot: the roundhouse requires the supporting foot to pivot outward as the hip rotates into the kick. Under fatigue this pivot disappears first — watch for it.
Tactical note: the roundhouse to the body targets the ribs or liver. After two punches that have pulled the guard inward, the body is exposed. This is the combination logic.
If the roundhouse loses its pivot and hip rotation after the punches, drop to 60% effort and rebuild. A kick without hip rotation is just a leg swing and will load the knee joint incorrectly on a heavy bag.
Jab-cross → mawashi geri → land → reset → repeat.
7
Free Round — Full Atemi-Waza Toolkit
No prescribed combination. Use everything — jab, cross, hooks, mae geri, mawashi geri — in whatever sequence feels natural and reactive.
Stay tactical: vary your entries. Sometimes punch first, sometimes kick first. Change levels — body kicks after head punches, or vice versa. Keep the bag guessing.
Guard rule: every combination ends with both hands at cheek height. No exceptions, even when tired.
8
Free Round — Finish Strong
Same as Round 7. This is the hardest round of the mesocycle so far.
Final 30 sec: give everything you have. Guard up, hips active, move after every combination. Finish the session the way you want to finish the cycle.

Universal Correction — All Strikes
  • Shoulder blade down and forward: cross, lead hook, rear hook — push the shoulder blade down as each punch launches. Do not let it ride up toward the ear. This is the fix for left trap overactivation and arm-too-far-from-body on all strikes.
  • Hip reset between consecutive same-side strikes: heel returns to floor between two crosses or two same-side kicks. Each strike is a full rotation and a full recovery.
  • Guard stays up during kicks: the kick does not excuse the hands from their position. Both fists at cheek height during every kick, every round, regardless of fatigue.
  • Chamber first on every kick: the quality of the kick is determined by the chamber. Rushed chamber = weak, unstable kick.
